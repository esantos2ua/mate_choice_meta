Introduction & Methodology

Publication bias is a critical threat to the validity of meta-analytic syntheses. If studies with statistically non-significant or small effects are systematically omitted from the literature, the pooled meta-analytic summary will be biased upward.

To thoroughly evaluate the presence of publication bias and report-level selection in our Mate-Choice Copying (MCC) dataset, we employ three complementary diagnostic approaches, keeping the exact multilevel modeling logic (e.g., nested random intercepts and taxonomy control) used in our main overall effect calculations:

  1. Visual Inspection (Funnel Plots): We plot effect size estimates against their precision (inverse of the standard error, \(1/\text{SE}\)) to check for visual asymmetry around the overall mean estimate.
  2. Egger’s Meta-Regression (Small-Study Effects): We test whether study precision significantly moderates effect size. Following the recommendations of Nakagawa et al. (2022) and Stanley (2012, 2014), we implement a two-step approach:
    • We first fit the Egger regression with the standard error (\(\text{SE} = \sqrt{v_i}\)) as the moderator. The slope tests for small-study effects, and the intercept estimates the effect that a perfectly precise study (\(\text{SE} = 0\)) would report.
    • When that intercept is statistically significant — i.e., a genuine non-zero effect is likely present — the standard-error version is known to under-estimate the true effect. We therefore re-fit the regression with the sampling variance (\(\text{V} = v_i\)) as the moderator, which gives a less biased estimate of the bias-adjusted overall mean.
  3. Decline Effect / Time-Lag Bias: We test whether effect sizes shrink over time by adding publication year (mean-centered) to our models while simultaneously controlling for study precision.
  4. Bias-Corrected Meta-Analytic Means (Method Comparison): Finally, we estimate the adjusted overall mean under two complementary correction frameworks and compare them directly. The current approach follows Nakagawa et al. (2022): the intercept of a multilevel meta-regression on the sampling variance (\(v_i\)) is interpreted as the mean when sampling error is zero (i.e., an infinitely precise study). The new approach follows Yang et al. (2024): a bias-robust weighting scheme (a fixed-/common-effect GLS estimator that is less susceptible to selective reporting) is combined with cluster-robust (CR2) variance estimation to handle the non-independence of effect sizes within studies. We overlay both corrected means on an orchard plot using orchaRd::pub_bias_plot().

NoteReading the funnel plots

Both panels plot each effect size against its precision (\(1/\text{SE}\)), but differ in what is removed before plotting:

  • (a) Marginal residuals are the deviations of each observed effect from the fixed-effect (intercept) prediction — the pooled mean — so they still carry all of the random-effects heterogeneity (between-study, between-effect-size, and between-species). Asymmetry here — a shortage of small, imprecise studies on one side of the funnel — is the classic visual signature of small-study and publication bias. Because genuine heterogeneity can mimic or exaggerate that asymmetry, apparent gaps in (a) should be read cautiously and confirmed against the formal Egger’s regression below.
  • (b) Conditional residuals additionally subtract the estimated random intercepts (study, effect-size, and species BLUPs), so the heterogeneity captured by the multilevel structure is partialled out. A symmetric, evenly-filled funnel here means that once the nesting is accounted for there is little precision-related asymmetry left — i.e., the asymmetry seen in (a) is largely explained by the modelled random effects rather than by selective reporting. Any asymmetry that persists in (b) is the more credible visual indication of true small-study effects.

2. Egger’s Meta-Regression (Small-Study Effects)

To perform Egger’s regression on Hedges’ \(g\), we follow the two-step framework recommended by Nakagawa et al. (2022) and Stanley (2012, 2014). We use the sampling standard error (\(\text{SE} = \sqrt{v_i}\)) as our precision proxy. If this moderator is statistically significant (\(p < 0.05\)), it indicates a significant small-study effect (larger standard errors / smaller sample sizes associated with larger effect magnitudes).

When the intercept of the standard-error model is significant (a genuine non-zero effect is likely present), that intercept tends to under-estimate the true effect. We therefore apply the second step: re-fit Egger’s regression with the sampling variance (\(\text{V} = \text{vi}\)) as the moderator, whose intercept gives a less biased estimate of the bias-adjusted overall mean.

2. Egger’s Meta-Regression (Small-Study Effects)

For Odds Ratio studies, we use the standard error (\(\text{SE} = \sqrt{v_i}\)) as our precision proxy in the two-step framework. If the standard error moderator slope is statistically significant (\(p < 0.05\)), it represents a significant small-study effect.

Bias-Corrected Meta-Analytic Means: Current vs. New Method

The diagnostics above detect small-study and time-lag bias. Here we turn to correcting the pooled mean, and we directly compare two correction philosophies on the same data.

  • Current method — Nakagawa et al. (2022) regression correction. We re-use the multilevel meta-regression that includes the sampling variance (\(v_i\)) as a moderator (the Step-2 models egger_hedges_v / egger_or_v fitted above). Its intercept estimates the meta-analytic mean extrapolated to \(v_i = 0\) — i.e., the effect a hypothetical infinitely-precise (infinite-\(n\)) study would report, with the small-study component removed. This is the “bias-corrected estimate” in Yang et al. (2024)’s terminology and in orchaRd.
  • New method — Yang et al. (2024) bias-robust estimation. A two-step procedure: (1) fit a fixed-/common-effect model, whose precision-based GLS weighting is far less inflated by selectively-reported small studies than the random-effects mean; then (2) apply cluster-robust (CR2) variance estimation (clubSandwich), clustering by study, so that the standard errors and confidence intervals remain valid under the non-independence of multiple effect sizes per study. This is the “bias-robust estimate”.

We compute both, overlay them on an orchard plot of the uncorrected multilevel mean with orchaRd::pub_bias_plot(), and tabulate the three estimates (uncorrected vs. the two corrections) side by side.

## Introduction & Methodology

Publication bias is a critical threat to the validity of meta-analytic syntheses. If studies with statistically non-significant or small effects are systematically omitted from the literature, the pooled meta-analytic summary will be biased upward.

To thoroughly evaluate the presence of publication bias and report-level selection in our Mate-Choice Copying (MCC) dataset, we employ three complementary diagnostic approaches, keeping the exact multilevel modeling logic (e.g., nested random intercepts and taxonomy control) used in our main overall effect calculations:

1. **Visual Inspection (Funnel Plots):** We plot effect size estimates against their precision (inverse of the standard error, $1/\text{SE}$) to check for visual asymmetry around the overall mean estimate.
2. **Egger's Meta-Regression (Small-Study Effects):** We test whether study precision significantly moderates effect size. Following the recommendations of @nakagawaMethodsTestingPublication2022 and Stanley [-@stanleyMetaregressionAnalysisEconomics2012; -@stanleyMetaregressionApproximationsReduce2014], we implement a **two-step approach**:
   * We first fit the Egger regression with the standard error ($\text{SE} = \sqrt{v_i}$) as the moderator. The slope tests for small-study effects, and the intercept estimates the effect that a perfectly precise study ($\text{SE} = 0$) would report.
   * When that intercept is statistically significant — i.e., a genuine non-zero effect is likely present — the standard-error version is known to under-estimate the true effect. We therefore re-fit the regression with the sampling variance ($\text{V} = v_i$) as the moderator, which gives a less biased estimate of the bias-adjusted overall mean.
3. **Decline Effect / Time-Lag Bias:** We test whether effect sizes shrink over time by adding publication year (mean-centered) to our models while simultaneously controlling for study precision.
4. **Bias-Corrected Meta-Analytic Means (Method Comparison):** Finally, we estimate the *adjusted* overall mean under two complementary correction frameworks and compare them directly. The **current** approach follows @nakagawaMethodsTestingPublication2022: the intercept of a multilevel meta-regression on the sampling variance ($v_i$) is interpreted as the mean when sampling error is zero (i.e., an infinitely precise study). The **new** approach follows @yangRobustPointVariance2024: a *bias-robust* weighting scheme (a fixed-/common-effect GLS estimator that is less susceptible to selective reporting) is combined with **cluster-robust (CR2) variance estimation** to handle the non-independence of effect sizes within studies. We overlay both corrected means on an orchard plot using `orchaRd::pub_bias_plot()`.

#### 1. Baseline Model & Funnel Plot

We run the intercept-only model to establish the global mean effect size and plot precision ($1/\text{SE}$) against the standardized residuals.

```{r}
#| label: hedges_baseline_and_funnel
#| message: false
#| warning: false
#| fig.width: 12
#| fig.height: 6
# Intercept-only model
VCV_hedges <- metafor::vcalc(vi = vi, cluster = identifierStudyId, obs = identifierEffectSizeID, rho = 0.5, data = db_hedges)

ma_hedges <- metafor::rma.mv(
    yi = yi,
    V = VCV_hedges,
    random = list(~ 1 | identifierStudyId, ~ 1 | identifierEffectSizeID, ~ 1 | taxonomySpecies),
    test = "t",
    method = "REML",
    sparse = TRUE,
    data = db_hedges
)

summary(ma_hedges)

# Extract BLUPs for random effects
blups <- ranef(ma_hedges)
study_ef <- blups$identifierStudyId$intrcpt[match(db_hedges$identifierStudyId, row.names(blups$identifierStudyId))]
obs_ef <- blups$identifierEffectSizeID$intrcpt[match(db_hedges$identifierEffectSizeID, row.names(blups$identifierEffectSizeID))]
spec_ef <- blups$taxonomySpecies$intrcpt[match(db_hedges$taxonomySpecies, row.names(blups$taxonomySpecies))]

# Handle any missing values by replacing with 0
study_ef[is.na(study_ef)] <- 0
obs_ef[is.na(obs_ef)] <- 0
spec_ef[is.na(spec_ef)] <- 0

# Marginal residuals: observed - fixed-effect prediction
resid_marg <- db_hedges$yi - predict(ma_hedges)[[1]]

# Conditional residuals: observed - fixed - random intercepts (study, obs, species)
resid_cond <- db_hedges$yi - (predict(ma_hedges)[[1]] + study_ef + obs_ef + spec_ef)

# Side-by-side funnel plots of marginal vs. conditional residuals,
# with (a)/(b) panel tags. Drawn on-screen and saved to a manuscript PDF.
draw_funnels(resid_marg, resid_cond, db_hedges$vi)
save_base_pdf(
    "funnel_hedges",
    function() draw_funnels(resid_marg, resid_cond, db_hedges$vi)
)
```

::: {.callout-note title="Reading the funnel plots"}
Both panels plot each effect size against its precision ($1/\text{SE}$), but differ in what is removed before plotting:

* **(a) Marginal residuals** are the deviations of each observed effect from the *fixed-effect (intercept) prediction* — the pooled mean — so they still carry all of the random-effects heterogeneity (between-study, between-effect-size, and between-species). Asymmetry here — a shortage of small, imprecise studies on one side of the funnel — is the classic visual signature of small-study and publication bias. Because genuine heterogeneity can mimic or exaggerate that asymmetry, apparent gaps in (a) should be read cautiously and confirmed against the formal Egger's regression below.
* **(b) Conditional residuals** additionally subtract the estimated random intercepts (study, effect-size, and species BLUPs), so the heterogeneity captured by the multilevel structure is partialled out. A symmetric, evenly-filled funnel here means that once the nesting is accounted for there is little precision-related asymmetry left — i.e., the asymmetry seen in (a) is largely explained by the modelled random effects rather than by selective reporting. Any asymmetry that *persists* in (b) is the more credible visual indication of true small-study effects.
:::

#### 2. Egger's Meta-Regression (Small-Study Effects)

To perform Egger's regression on Hedges' $g$, we follow the two-step framework recommended by @nakagawaMethodsTestingPublication2022 and Stanley [-@stanleyMetaregressionAnalysisEconomics2012; -@stanleyMetaregressionApproximationsReduce2014]. We use the sampling standard error ($\text{SE} = \sqrt{v_i}$) as our precision proxy. If this moderator is statistically significant ($p < 0.05$), it indicates a significant small-study effect (larger standard errors / smaller sample sizes associated with larger effect magnitudes).

When the intercept of the standard-error model is significant (a genuine non-zero effect is likely present), that intercept tends to under-estimate the true effect. We therefore apply the second step: re-fit Egger's regression with the sampling variance ($\text{V} = \text{vi}$) as the moderator, whose intercept gives a less biased estimate of the bias-adjusted overall mean.

```{r}
#| label: hedges_eggers_regression
#| message: false
#| warning: false
# Calculate standard error precision proxy
db_hedges_egger <- db_hedges %>%
    mutate(se = sqrt(vi)) %>%
    filter(!is.na(se))

# Re-estimate VCV on this subset
VCV_hedges_egger <- metafor::vcalc(vi = vi, cluster = identifierStudyId, obs = identifierEffectSizeID, rho = 0.5, data = db_hedges_egger)

# Step 1: Egger regression model with standard error as moderator
egger_hedges <- metafor::rma.mv(
    yi = yi,
    V = VCV_hedges_egger,
    mods = ~ 1 + se,
    random = list(~ 1 | identifierStudyId, ~ 1 | identifierEffectSizeID, ~ 1 | taxonomySpecies),
    test = "t",
    method = "REML",
    sparse = TRUE,
    data = db_hedges_egger
)

summary(egger_hedges)

# Step 2: Since the intercept is significant, re-fit using variance (vi)
egger_hedges_v <- metafor::rma.mv(
    yi = yi,
    V = VCV_hedges_egger,
    mods = ~ 1 + vi,
    random = list(~ 1 | identifierStudyId, ~ 1 | identifierEffectSizeID, ~ 1 | taxonomySpecies),
    test = "t",
    method = "REML",
    sparse = TRUE,
    data = db_hedges_egger
)

summary(egger_hedges_v)
```

::: {.callout-note title="Reading the funnel plots"}
Both panels plot each $\ln\text{OR}$ effect size against its precision ($1/\text{SE}$), but differ in what is removed before plotting:

* **(a) Marginal residuals** are the deviations of each observed effect from the *fixed-effect (intercept) prediction* — the pooled mean — so they still carry all of the random-effects heterogeneity (between-study, between-effect-size, and between-species). Asymmetry here — a shortage of small, imprecise studies on one side of the funnel — is the classic visual signature of small-study and publication bias. Because genuine heterogeneity can mimic or exaggerate that asymmetry, apparent gaps in (a) should be read cautiously and confirmed against the formal Egger's regression below.
* **(b) Conditional residuals** additionally subtract the estimated random intercepts (study, effect-size, and species BLUPs), so the heterogeneity captured by the multilevel structure is partialled out. A symmetric, evenly-filled funnel here means that once the nesting is accounted for there is little precision-related asymmetry left — i.e., the asymmetry seen in (a) is largely explained by the modelled random effects rather than by selective reporting. Any asymmetry that *persists* in (b) is the more credible visual indication of true small-study effects.
:::

#### 2. Egger's Meta-Regression (Small-Study Effects)

For Odds Ratio studies, we use the standard error ($\text{SE} = \sqrt{v_i}$) as our precision proxy in the two-step framework. If the standard error moderator slope is statistically significant ($p < 0.05$), it represents a significant small-study effect.

As with the continuous outcomes, we apply the second step when the intercept of the standard-error model is significant: we re-fit Egger's regression with the sampling variance ($\text{V} = \text{vi}$) as the moderator, whose intercept gives a less biased estimate of the bias-adjusted overall mean.

```{r}
#| label: or_eggers_regression
#| message: false
#| warning: false

    mutate(se = sqrt(vi)) %>%

    yi = yi,
```

## Bias-Corrected Meta-Analytic Means: Current vs. New Method

The diagnostics above *detect* small-study and time-lag bias. Here we turn to *correcting* the pooled mean, and we directly compare two correction philosophies on the same data.

* **Current method — Nakagawa et al. [-@nakagawaMethodsTestingPublication2022] regression correction.** We re-use the multilevel meta-regression that includes the sampling variance ($v_i$) as a moderator (the Step-2 models `egger_hedges_v` / `egger_or_v` fitted above). Its **intercept** estimates the meta-analytic mean extrapolated to $v_i = 0$ — i.e., the effect a hypothetical infinitely-precise (infinite-$n$) study would report, with the small-study component removed. This is the *"bias-corrected estimate"* in @yangRobustPointVariance2024's terminology and in `orchaRd`.
* **New method — Yang et al. [-@yangRobustPointVariance2024] bias-robust estimation.** A two-step procedure: (1) fit a **fixed-/common-effect** model, whose precision-based GLS weighting is far less inflated by selectively-reported small studies than the random-effects mean; then (2) apply **cluster-robust (CR2) variance estimation** (`clubSandwich`), clustering by study, so that the standard errors and confidence intervals remain valid under the non-independence of multiple effect sizes per study. This is the *"bias-robust estimate"*.

We compute both, overlay them on an orchard plot of the uncorrected multilevel mean with `orchaRd::pub_bias_plot()`, and tabulate the three estimates (uncorrected vs. the two corrections) side by side.

::: {.panel-tabset}
