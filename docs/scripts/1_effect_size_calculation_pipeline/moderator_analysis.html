Why a Moderator Analysis?

The overall synthesis (Heterogeneity Decomposition) showed that the updated evidence base is dominated by persistent, largely unexplained heterogeneity. Total \(I^2\) was high across every model, yet very little of that variance sat at the study or phylogenetic level. Instead it accumulated at the within-study (observation) and non-phylogenetic species levels — exactly where experimental and ecological distinctions among individual trials live. The disagreement among mate-choice copying estimates is therefore not mainly a matter of some laboratories producing systematically larger effects, nor of copying being concentrated in particular lineages; it points to genuine biological and methodological moderators that the intercept-only models could not capture.

During extraction we collected several candidate moderators that are plausible sources of this within-study/within-species variation:

  • Copying mechanism — generalized (copying a type of male) versus individual (copying a specific demonstrated male);
  • Modality of the social cue — the sensory channel of the demonstration (visual, chemical, or acoustic);
  • Virginity of the observer — whether the choosing individual was sexually naïve;
  • Relative age of the demonstrator — the age of the demonstrator relative to the observer.

This chapter does two things, in order:

  1. Diagnostics (Part 1). Before fitting anything, we audit each candidate moderator in the new extraction — how its levels are populated, how much is missing, and how many studies/species back each level — and we check whether the coding can be reconciled with the original meta-analyses. This determines which moderators are analysable and on which dataset (new-only versus combined).
  2. Uni-moderator models (Part 2). For the moderators that pass the diagnostic, we fit single-moderator multilevel models using the same rma.mv machinery, omnibus \(Q_M\) test, and partial-\(I^2\) heterogeneity decomposition used elsewhere in this workflow. We report two things for each: (i) whether the moderator explains a meaningful share of the heterogeneity (the \(Q_M\) / \(I^2\) question), and (ii) the direction and magnitude of the differences between levels (§Biologically Meaningful Level Differences) — because even when a contrast is not statistically distinct, its sign can speak to specific hypotheses raised in the main text (most notably whether virgin observers copy more, as the original synthesis reported).

These analyses are exploratory. With the dataset at its current size, several moderator levels are thin, and we have not pre-registered these tests. We report them to map the most promising directions for future, ideally pre-registered, syntheses — not as confirmatory claims.

Part 1 — Moderator Diagnostics

Candidate moderators and their extraction columns

Coverage in the new extraction

For each candidate moderator we tabulate, per level, the number of effect sizes (k), the number of distinct studies and species backing that level, and the percentage of effect sizes with a missing value. A moderator is only worth modelling if it varies, if each level is backed by more than one study (so the moderator is not perfectly confounded with a single study), and if missingness is low.

The social-cue modality recode

The extraction column experimentalTypeStimulus records the form of the demonstration rather than a sensory label directly, so we map it onto a modality:

experimentalTypeStimulus Sensory modality
Copulation, Association, Presence of eggs in nest Visual
Odour stimuli Chemical
Acoustic stimuli Acoustic

The coverage table makes the central limitation plain: the new extraction is overwhelmingly visual, with the Chemical and Acoustic levels each resting on a handful of effect sizes from very few studies. A three-level modality model can be fitted, but its non-visual contrasts are effectively confounded with one or two studies/species and must be read with extreme caution. We therefore also define demo_type — Copulation versus Association, the two well-populated visual sub-types — as a better-powered sensitivity contrast within the dominant modality.

Harmonisation with the original meta-analyses

The four moderators were coded under a single protocol in the new extraction, but the original datasets recorded only some of them, and not always on a compatible scale. The table below records, for each moderator, whether a compatible variable exists in Davies et al. 2020 (Hedges’ \(g\)) and Jones & DuVal 2019 (log OR), and the resulting verdict.

Diagnostic verdict

Putting the coverage and harmonisation evidence together:

  • Copying mechanism (Generalized vs Individual) — well balanced in the new extraction (37 vs 32 effect sizes, each from many studies) and coded identically by Davies. Analysable new-only on both scales, and combinable on the Hedges’ \(g\) scale.
  • Observer virginity (No vs Yes) — both levels well populated (21 vs 48) and coded identically by Jones & DuVal. Analysable new-only on both scales, and combinable on the log OR scale.
  • Social-cue modality (Visual/Chemical/Acoustic) — analysable new-only, but the non-visual levels are sparse and study-confounded; treated as exploratory, with the Copulation-vs-Association visual contrast as a sensitivity check. The original “demonstration type” codes a different construct and cannot be merged.
  • Demonstrator relative age — dropped: the new extraction records 1 non-missing level(s) (“Same-age”), i.e. essentially no variation, so it cannot enter a model. The original datasets carry no compatible variable.

Bottom line. Three moderators (mechanism, modality, virginity) can be tested on the new dataset for both effect-size scales. Two of them can additionally be tested on a combined dataset where the construct harmonises cleanly — mechanism on Hedges’ \(g\) (new + Davies) and virginity on log OR (new + Jones). Demonstrator age is not analysable. Part 2 implements exactly these models.

Part 2 — Uni-Moderator Models

Modelling approach

Each model is a single-moderator multilevel meta-analysis fitted with metafor::rma.mv(), mirroring the taxonomic-moderator model:

  • Dependency. A variance–covariance matrix from vcalc() (\(\rho = 0.5\)) handles multiple effect sizes within a study.
  • Random effects. Study (identifierStudyId), residual effect-size (identifierEffectSizeID), and a non-phylogenetic species intercept (taxonomySpecies). The phylogenetic term is omitted: the heterogeneity decomposition showed it carries negligible variance, and dropping it keeps these smaller moderator models identifiable (the same choice made for the taxonomic model).
  • Heteroscedasticity with a fallback cascade. We first attempt a heteroscedastic structure that lets the residual (within-study) variance differ by moderator level (~ moderator | effect-size, struct = "HCS", rho = 0), trying several optimisers; if it errors we fall back to a homoscedastic structure. This is the orchaRd 2.0 recommendation for meta-regression with heterogeneous variances (Nakagawa et al. 2023).
  • Inference. The moderator is intercept-coded (mods = ~ moderator); per-level means, CIs, and prediction intervals come from orchaRd::mod_results() (marginalised means via emmeans), and the among-level test is the model’s omnibus \(Q_M\). Heterogeneity is decomposed with partial \(I^2\) from orchaRd::i2_ml().

# Generalised heteroscedastic single-moderator fitter (adapted from
# fit_taxon_hetero_mv in overall_effect.qmd). Returns the chosen model plus a
# cascade log so the reader can see which structure was actually used.
fit_mod_hetero <- function(dat, group_var, group_levels, yi = "yi", vi = "vi",
                           cluster_var = "identifierStudyId",
                           obs_var = "identifierEffectSizeID", rho = 0.5) {
    dat2 <- dat %>%
        filter(!is.na(.data[[yi]]), !is.na(.data[[vi]]), .data[[vi]] > 0, !is.na(.data[[group_var]]))
    if (nrow(dat2) == 0) return(NULL)
    dat2[[group_var]] <- droplevels(factor(dat2[[group_var]], levels = group_levels))

    VCV <- metafor::vcalc(vi = dat2[[vi]], cluster = dat2[[cluster_var]], obs = dat2[[obs_var]], rho = rho, data = dat2)
    mods_f <- stats::as.formula(paste0("~ ", group_var))
    obs_f  <- stats::as.formula(paste0("~ ", group_var, " | ", obs_var))

    opt_grid <- list(
        "nlminb"            = list(optimizer = "nlminb", iter.max = 10000, eval.max = 10000),
        "optim/BFGS"        = list(optimizer = "optim", optmethod = "BFGS", maxit = 10000),
        "optim/L-BFGS-B"    = list(optimizer = "optim", optmethod = "L-BFGS-B", maxit = 10000),
        "optim/Nelder-Mead" = list(optimizer = "optim", optmethod = "Nelder-Mead", maxit = 50000, reltol = 1e-9)
    )
    build <- function(cand, ctrl) {
        args <- c(
            list(yi = dat2[[yi]], V = VCV, mods = mods_f, random = cand$random,
                 test = "t", dfs = "contain", method = "REML", sparse = TRUE, data = dat2),
            cand$args
        )
        if (!is.null(cand$struct)) args$struct <- cand$struct
        if (!is.null(ctrl)) args$control <- ctrl
        do.call(metafor::rma.mv, args)
    }
    candidates <- list(
        list(spec = "Heteroscedastic (residual only, per level; HCS, rho = 0)",
             random = list(~ 1 | identifierStudyId, obs_f, ~ 1 | taxonomySpecies),
             struct = "HCS", args = list(rho = 0), het = TRUE),
        list(spec = "Homoscedastic (single variance per component)",
             random = list(~ 1 | identifierStudyId, ~ 1 | identifierEffectSizeID, ~ 1 | taxonomySpecies),
             struct = NULL, args = list(), het = FALSE)
    )
    fit_once <- function(cand, ctrl) {
        warns <- character(0); t0 <- Sys.time()
        mod <- tryCatch(
            withCallingHandlers(build(cand, ctrl),
                warning = function(w) { warns[[length(warns) + 1L]] <<- conditionMessage(w); invokeRestart("muffleWarning") }),
            error = function(e) structure(list(msg = conditionMessage(e)), class = "mod_fit_error")
        )
        list(mod = mod, warns = warns, secs = as.numeric(difftime(Sys.time(), t0, units = "secs")))
    }
    attempt <- function(cand) {
        if (!isTRUE(cand$het)) {
            r <- fit_once(cand, NULL); failed <- inherits(r$mod, "mod_fit_error")
            return(list(model = if (failed) NULL else r$mod, failed = failed,
                        error = if (failed) r$mod$msg else NA_character_, warnings = r$warns,
                        secs = r$secs, optimizer = "default (nlminb)"))
        }
        msgs <- character(0); total <- 0
        for (onm in names(opt_grid)) {
            r <- fit_once(cand, opt_grid[[onm]]); total <- total + r$secs
            if (!inherits(r$mod, "mod_fit_error")) {
                return(list(model = r$mod, failed = FALSE, error = NA_character_,
                            warnings = r$warns, secs = total, optimizer = onm))
            }
            msgs <- c(msgs, paste0(onm, ": ", r$mod$msg))
        }
        list(model = NULL, failed = TRUE, error = paste(msgs, collapse = " || "),
             warnings = character(0), secs = total, optimizer = NA_character_)
    }
    diag_rows <- list(); chosen <- NULL; chosen_spec <- NA_character_
    for (i in seq_along(candidates)) {
        cand <- candidates[[i]]
        if (!is.null(chosen)) {
            diag_rows[[i]] <- tibble::tibble(stage = i, structure = cand$spec, status = "not attempted",
                optimizer = NA_character_, seconds = NA_real_, n_warnings = NA_integer_,
                detail = "an earlier structure succeeded")
            next
        }
        a <- attempt(cand)
        status <- if (a$failed) "ERROR" else if (length(a$warnings) > 0) "OK (with warnings)" else "OK"
        detail <- if (a$failed) a$error else if (length(a$warnings) > 0) paste(a$warnings, collapse = " || ") else "clean fit"
        diag_rows[[i]] <- tibble::tibble(stage = i, structure = cand$spec, status = status,
            optimizer = a$optimizer, seconds = round(a$secs, 2), n_warnings = length(a$warnings), detail = detail)
        if (!a$failed) { chosen <- a$model; chosen_spec <- cand$spec }
    }
    diagnostics <- dplyr::bind_rows(diag_rows)
    if (is.null(chosen)) {
        warning("All structures failed for moderator '", group_var, "'.")
        return(list(data = dat2, VCV = VCV, model = NULL, spec = "ALL FAILED",
                    diagnostics = diagnostics, group_var = group_var))
    }
    list(data = dat2, VCV = VCV, model = chosen, spec = chosen_spec,
         diagnostics = diagnostics, group_var = group_var)
}

# Partial I^2 (long form) for a fitted moderator model.
comp_labels <- c(
    Total                  = "Total",
    identifierStudyId      = "Study",
    identifierEffectSizeID = "Effect size (residual)",
    taxonomySpecies        = "Species (non-phylogenetic)"
)
# orchaRd::i2_ml() does not support the heteroscedastic (HCS) structure that the
# cascade prefers, so for the partial-I² *decomposition* we compute i2_ml on a
# homoscedastic refit of the same data (one variance per random effect). This is
# exactly how the overall synthesis reports partial I² (from the homoscedastic
# intercept models); the heteroscedastic model is retained for the means/Q_M.
i2_homosc_refit <- function(res) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model) || is.null(res$data)) return(NULL)
    d <- res$data
    m <- tryCatch(
        metafor::rma.mv(
            yi = d$yi, V = res$VCV,
            mods = stats::as.formula(paste0("~ ", res$group_var)),
            random = list(~ 1 | identifierStudyId, ~ 1 | identifierEffectSizeID, ~ 1 | taxonomySpecies),
            test = "t", method = "REML", sparse = TRUE, data = d
        ),
        error = function(e) NULL
    )
    if (is.null(m)) return(NULL)
    tryCatch(orchaRd::i2_ml(m), error = function(e) NULL)
}

get_i2_long <- function(res, analysis) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model)) return(NULL)
    v <- tryCatch(orchaRd::i2_ml(res$model), error = function(e) NULL)
    if (is.null(v)) v <- i2_homosc_refit(res)
    if (is.null(v)) return(NULL)
    tibble::tibble(Analysis = analysis, Component = sub("^I2_", "", names(v)), I2 = round(as.numeric(v), 1)) %>%
        mutate(Component = dplyr::recode(Component, !!!comp_labels))
}

# Total I^2: orchaRd on the fitted model, else on the homoscedastic refit (i2_ml
# does not support the HCS structure), else a Higgins typical-variance fallback.
total_i2 <- function(res) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model)) return(NA_real_)
    i2 <- tryCatch(orchaRd::i2_ml(res$model), error = function(e) NULL)
    if (is.null(i2)) i2 <- i2_homosc_refit(res)
    if (!is.null(i2) && any(grepl("Total", names(i2)))) return(as.numeric(i2[grep("Total", names(i2))[1]]))
    m <- res$model; vi <- res$data$vi; wi <- 1 / vi; k <- length(vi)
    s2 <- (k - 1) * sum(wi) / (sum(wi)^2 - sum(wi^2))
    vc <- sum(c(m$sigma2, m$tau2, m$gamma2), na.rm = TRUE)
    100 * vc / (vc + s2)
}

# Per-level marginalised means table.
level_means <- function(res, analysis) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model)) return(NULL)
    mr <- orchaRd::mod_results(res$model, mod = res$group_var, group = "identifierStudyId")
    mr$mod_table %>%
        transmute(Analysis = analysis, Level = name,
                  Estimate = round(estimate, 3),
                  `CI lower` = round(lowerCL, 3), `CI upper` = round(upperCL, 3),
                  `PI lower` = round(lowerPR, 3), `PI upper` = round(upperPR, 3))
}

# Between-level contrasts: each non-intercept model coefficient is the
# difference between that level and the reference level, on the effect-size
# scale. A statistically "distinct" difference would have a CI excluding zero;
# a biologically suggestive one is read from the sign and magnitude even when the
# CI overlaps zero. For log OR models we also report the multiplicative odds
# ratio (exp(difference)) so the contrast is interpretable on the OR scale.
level_contrasts <- function(res, analysis, is_or = FALSE) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model)) return(NULL)
    m <- res$model
    nm <- rownames(m$b)
    keep <- !grepl("intrcpt|intercept", nm, ignore.case = TRUE)
    if (!any(keep)) return(NULL)
    ref <- levels(droplevels(factor(res$data[[res$group_var]], levels = levels(res$data[[res$group_var]]))))[1]
    out <- tibble::tibble(
        Analysis = analysis,
        Contrast = paste0(sub(res$group_var, "", nm[keep]), " − ", ref),
        Difference = round(as.numeric(m$b)[keep], 3),
        `CI lower` = round(m$ci.lb[keep], 3),
        `CI upper` = round(m$ci.ub[keep], 3),
        p = round(m$pval[keep], 3)
    )
    if (is_or) {
        out$`Odds ratio` <- round(exp(out$Difference), 2)
        out <- dplyr::relocate(out, `Odds ratio`, .after = Difference)
    }
    out
}

Biologically Meaningful Level Differences

A non-significant \(Q_M\) tells us a moderator does not explain the heterogeneity, but it does not tell us whether the direction and magnitude of the level differences are biologically interesting. Because the point estimates speak to specific hypotheses raised in the main text, we read them here as well — keeping firmly in mind that every contrast CI below overlaps zero, so these are directional signals, not demonstrated effects.

Observer virginity — a signal that runs against the original meta-analysis

This is the most noteworthy contrast. Jones & DuVal (Jones and DuVal 2019) reported — and the present manuscript’s discussion repeats as support for the “copy when inexperienced” social-learning strategy — that virgin (sexually naïve) observers copy more strongly. Our new extraction shows the opposite ordering: virgin observers copied less than non-virgins (Hedges’ \(g\) = 0.09 for virgins vs 0.36 for non-virgins; log OR = 0.16 vs 0.66). The difference is not statistically distinct (CIs overlap zero), but the sign is reversed relative to the classic result. When the new data are pooled with the original Jones & DuVal binary data, the contrast all but disappears (non-virgin OR = 1.91 vs virgin OR = 1.75).

The takeaway is not that the original finding is wrong, but that the updated, broader evidence base no longer reproduces the virgin-copies-more pattern, and the new studies, if anything, point the other way. This is precisely the kind of context-dependence the discussion invokes, and it argues for treating the virginity-conditionality claim with more caution than the original synthesis implied.

Demonstration cue: copulation versus mere association

Within the dominant visual modality, restricting to the two well-populated cue types shows copying trends stronger when the social cue is a copulation than mere association (Hedges’ \(g\) = 0.36 vs 0.11). This is the biologically expected direction — a completed copulation is a less ambiguous, higher-value social signal than spatial proximity alone — and it offers a concrete, testable prediction for future work, even though the contrast is not significant here.

Copying mechanism: generalized copying is not the weaker form

The introduction motivates generalized copying (forming a preference for a phenotype rather than an individual) as the route by which mate-choice copying could drive the cultural transmission of preferences and shape sexual selection. Reassuringly for that argument, generalized copying is no weaker than individual copying in the combined data (Hedges’ \(g\) = 0.43 generalized vs 0.44 individual) — the evolutionarily consequential form of the behaviour is at least as strong as the individual-recognition form.

Social-cue modality: a sampling gap, not a result

The modality comparison (visual \(g\) = 0.22, chemical = 0.31, acoustic = 0.08) cannot be read biologically: the chemical and acoustic levels rest on a handful of effect sizes from one to three studies and are confounded with particular species. Its real value is as a map of where the evidence is missing — the mate-choice-copying literature is overwhelmingly visual, even though the field’s own inclusion criteria explicitly admit chemical and acoustic cues. Broadening sensory sampling is a clear opportunity.

Across every model, the pattern is the same: total heterogeneity remains high and no single moderator’s omnibus test is significant. This is a genuinely informative null. It tells us that the heterogeneity is not explained by the most obvious experimental and ecological axes available in the current data, and it sharpens the case made in the discussion: the moderators that matter are likely finer-grained (specific paradigms, demonstrator quality, ecological context) or interactive, and the dataset is not yet large or balanced enough — particularly for the non-visual modalities and for any moderator absent from the legacy datasets — to resolve them. We therefore present these as hypothesis-generating results and echo the discussion’s recommendation that future, ideally pre-registered, syntheses test these moderators explicitly as the evidence base grows.

That said, the null heterogeneity result and the directional level differences are two different readings of the same models, and the second is where the biological interest lies. The standout is observer virginity: the updated data do not reproduce — and trend against — the virgin-copies-more pattern of the original synthesis, a nuance worth carrying into the discussion even though the contrast is not statistically distinct.

NoteCaveats

These models are exploratory and were not pre-registered. Several levels (Chemical and Acoustic modality in particular) rest on very few studies and are confounded with individual species, so their estimates are unstable. Absence of a significant \(Q_M\) is not evidence that a moderator has no effect — only that the current data cannot resolve one. Finally, where the heteroscedastic (HCS) structure was used, orchaRd/metafor flag that prediction intervals for random-slope structures are approximate (the point estimates, confidence intervals, and \(Q_M\) are unaffected); the PI columns in the per-level tables should therefore be read as indicative only.

## Why a Moderator Analysis? {#sec-motivation}

The overall synthesis ([Heterogeneity Decomposition](overall_effect.qmd#sec-heterogeneity)) showed that the updated evidence base is dominated by **persistent, largely unexplained heterogeneity**. Total $I^2$ was high across every model, yet very little of that variance sat at the *study* or *phylogenetic* level. Instead it accumulated at the **within-study (observation)** and **non-phylogenetic species** levels — exactly where experimental and ecological distinctions among individual trials live. The disagreement among mate-choice copying estimates is therefore not mainly a matter of some laboratories producing systematically larger effects, nor of copying being concentrated in particular lineages; it points to **genuine biological and methodological moderators** that the intercept-only models could not capture.

During extraction we collected several candidate moderators that are plausible sources of this within-study/within-species variation:

* **Copying mechanism** — *generalized* (copying a *type* of male) versus *individual* (copying a *specific* demonstrated male);
* **Modality of the social cue** — the sensory channel of the demonstration (visual, chemical, or acoustic);
* **Virginity of the observer** — whether the choosing individual was sexually naïve;
* **Relative age of the demonstrator** — the age of the demonstrator relative to the observer.

This chapter does two things, in order:

1. **Diagnostics (Part 1).** Before fitting anything, we audit each candidate moderator *in the new extraction* — how its levels are populated, how much is missing, and how many studies/species back each level — and we check whether the coding can be reconciled with the original meta-analyses. This determines **which** moderators are analysable and **on which dataset** (new-only versus combined).
2. **Uni-moderator models (Part 2).** For the moderators that pass the diagnostic, we fit single-moderator multilevel models using the same `rma.mv` machinery, omnibus $Q_M$ test, and partial-$I^2$ heterogeneity decomposition used elsewhere in this workflow. We report two things for each: (i) whether the moderator explains a meaningful share of the *heterogeneity* (the $Q_M$ / $I^2$ question), and (ii) the **direction and magnitude of the differences between levels** ([§Biologically Meaningful Level Differences](#sec-biological)) — because even when a contrast is not statistically distinct, its sign can speak to specific hypotheses raised in the main text (most notably whether virgin observers copy more, as the original synthesis reported).

> These analyses are **exploratory**. With the dataset at its current size, several moderator levels are thin, and we have not pre-registered these tests. We report them to map the most promising directions for future, ideally pre-registered, syntheses — not as confirmatory claims.

### The social-cue modality recode {#sec-modality-recode}

The extraction column `experimentalTypeStimulus` records the *form of the demonstration* rather than a sensory label directly, so we map it onto a modality:

| `experimentalTypeStimulus` | Sensory modality |
|---|---|
| Copulation, Association, Presence of eggs in nest | **Visual** |
| Odour stimuli | **Chemical** |
| Acoustic stimuli | **Acoustic** |

The coverage table makes the central limitation plain: the new extraction is **overwhelmingly visual**, with the *Chemical* and *Acoustic* levels each resting on a handful of effect sizes from very few studies. A three-level modality model can be fitted, but its non-visual contrasts are effectively confounded with one or two studies/species and must be read with extreme caution. We therefore also define `demo_type` — *Copulation* versus *Association*, the two well-populated visual sub-types — as a better-powered sensitivity contrast within the dominant modality.

### Diagnostic verdict {#sec-verdict}

Putting the coverage and harmonisation evidence together:

* **Copying mechanism (Generalized vs Individual)** — well balanced in the new extraction (`r get_cov("Copying mechanism", "Generalized", "k (effect sizes)")` vs `r get_cov("Copying mechanism", "Individual", "k (effect sizes)")` effect sizes, each from many studies) and coded identically by Davies. **Analysable new-only on both scales, and combinable on the Hedges' $g$ scale.**
* **Observer virginity (No vs Yes)** — both levels well populated (`r get_cov("Observer virginity", "No", "k (effect sizes)")` vs `r get_cov("Observer virginity", "Yes", "k (effect sizes)")`) and coded identically by Jones & DuVal. **Analysable new-only on both scales, and combinable on the log OR scale.**
* **Social-cue modality (Visual/Chemical/Acoustic)** — analysable **new-only**, but the non-visual levels are sparse and study-confounded; treated as exploratory, with the Copulation-vs-Association visual contrast as a sensitivity check. The original "demonstration type" codes a different construct and cannot be merged.
* **Demonstrator relative age** — **dropped**: the new extraction records `r n_age_levels` non-missing level(s) ("Same-age"), i.e. essentially **no variation**, so it cannot enter a model. The original datasets carry no compatible variable.

**Bottom line.** Three moderators (mechanism, modality, virginity) can be tested on the **new dataset** for both effect-size scales. Two of them can additionally be tested on a **combined dataset** where the construct harmonises cleanly — mechanism on Hedges' $g$ (new + Davies) and virginity on log OR (new + Jones). Demonstrator age is not analysable. Part 2 implements exactly these models.

## Part 2 — Uni-Moderator Models {#sec-models}

### Modelling approach

Each model is a single-moderator multilevel meta-analysis fitted with `metafor::rma.mv()`, mirroring the [taxonomic-moderator model](overall_effect.qmd#sec-taxonomic-moderator):

* **Dependency.** A variance–covariance matrix from `vcalc()` ($\rho = 0.5$) handles multiple effect sizes within a study.
* **Random effects.** Study (`identifierStudyId`), residual effect-size (`identifierEffectSizeID`), and a non-phylogenetic species intercept (`taxonomySpecies`). The phylogenetic term is **omitted**: the heterogeneity decomposition showed it carries negligible variance, and dropping it keeps these smaller moderator models identifiable (the same choice made for the taxonomic model).
* **Heteroscedasticity with a fallback cascade.** We first attempt a heteroscedastic structure that lets the **residual (within-study) variance differ by moderator level** (`~ moderator | effect-size`, `struct = "HCS"`, `rho = 0`), trying several optimisers; if it errors we fall back to a homoscedastic structure. This is the `orchaRd` 2.0 recommendation for meta-regression with heterogeneous variances [@nakagawaOrchaRd2Visualising2023].
* **Inference.** The moderator is intercept-coded (`mods = ~ moderator`); per-level means, CIs, and prediction intervals come from `orchaRd::mod_results()` (marginalised means via `emmeans`), and the among-level test is the model's omnibus $Q_M$. Heterogeneity is decomposed with partial $I^2$ from `orchaRd::i2_ml()`.

```{r}
#| label: fit_function
#| message: false
#| warning: false
# Generalised heteroscedastic single-moderator fitter (adapted from
# fit_taxon_hetero_mv in overall_effect.qmd). Returns the chosen model plus a
# cascade log so the reader can see which structure was actually used.
fit_mod_hetero <- function(dat, group_var, group_levels, yi = "yi", vi = "vi",
                           cluster_var = "identifierStudyId",
                           obs_var = "identifierEffectSizeID", rho = 0.5) {
    dat2 <- dat %>%
        filter(!is.na(.data[[yi]]), !is.na(.data[[vi]]), .data[[vi]] > 0, !is.na(.data[[group_var]]))
    if (nrow(dat2) == 0) return(NULL)
    dat2[[group_var]] <- droplevels(factor(dat2[[group_var]], levels = group_levels))

    VCV <- metafor::vcalc(vi = dat2[[vi]], cluster = dat2[[cluster_var]], obs = dat2[[obs_var]], rho = rho, data = dat2)
    mods_f <- stats::as.formula(paste0("~ ", group_var))
    obs_f  <- stats::as.formula(paste0("~ ", group_var, " | ", obs_var))

    opt_grid <- list(
        "nlminb"            = list(optimizer = "nlminb", iter.max = 10000, eval.max = 10000),
        "optim/BFGS"        = list(optimizer = "optim", optmethod = "BFGS", maxit = 10000),
        "optim/L-BFGS-B"    = list(optimizer = "optim", optmethod = "L-BFGS-B", maxit = 10000),
        "optim/Nelder-Mead" = list(optimizer = "optim", optmethod = "Nelder-Mead", maxit = 50000, reltol = 1e-9)
    )
    build <- function(cand, ctrl) {
        args <- c(
            list(yi = dat2[[yi]], V = VCV, mods = mods_f, random = cand$random,
                 test = "t", dfs = "contain", method = "REML", sparse = TRUE, data = dat2),
            cand$args
        )
        if (!is.null(cand$struct)) args$struct <- cand$struct
        if (!is.null(ctrl)) args$control <- ctrl
        do.call(metafor::rma.mv, args)
    }
    candidates <- list(
        list(spec = "Heteroscedastic (residual only, per level; HCS, rho = 0)",
             random = list(~ 1 | identifierStudyId, obs_f, ~ 1 | taxonomySpecies),
             struct = "HCS", args = list(rho = 0), het = TRUE),
        list(spec = "Homoscedastic (single variance per component)",
             random = list(~ 1 | identifierStudyId, ~ 1 | identifierEffectSizeID, ~ 1 | taxonomySpecies),
             struct = NULL, args = list(), het = FALSE)
    )
    fit_once <- function(cand, ctrl) {
        warns <- character(0); t0 <- Sys.time()
        mod <- tryCatch(
            withCallingHandlers(build(cand, ctrl),
                warning = function(w) { warns[[length(warns) + 1L]] <<- conditionMessage(w); invokeRestart("muffleWarning") }),
            error = function(e) structure(list(msg = conditionMessage(e)), class = "mod_fit_error")
        )
        list(mod = mod, warns = warns, secs = as.numeric(difftime(Sys.time(), t0, units = "secs")))
    }
    attempt <- function(cand) {
        if (!isTRUE(cand$het)) {
            r <- fit_once(cand, NULL); failed <- inherits(r$mod, "mod_fit_error")
            return(list(model = if (failed) NULL else r$mod, failed = failed,
                        error = if (failed) r$mod$msg else NA_character_, warnings = r$warns,
                        secs = r$secs, optimizer = "default (nlminb)"))
        }
        msgs <- character(0); total <- 0
        for (onm in names(opt_grid)) {
            r <- fit_once(cand, opt_grid[[onm]]); total <- total + r$secs
            if (!inherits(r$mod, "mod_fit_error")) {
                return(list(model = r$mod, failed = FALSE, error = NA_character_,
                            warnings = r$warns, secs = total, optimizer = onm))
            }
            msgs <- c(msgs, paste0(onm, ": ", r$mod$msg))
        }
        list(model = NULL, failed = TRUE, error = paste(msgs, collapse = " || "),
             warnings = character(0), secs = total, optimizer = NA_character_)
    }
    diag_rows <- list(); chosen <- NULL; chosen_spec <- NA_character_
    for (i in seq_along(candidates)) {
        cand <- candidates[[i]]
        if (!is.null(chosen)) {
            diag_rows[[i]] <- tibble::tibble(stage = i, structure = cand$spec, status = "not attempted",
                optimizer = NA_character_, seconds = NA_real_, n_warnings = NA_integer_,
                detail = "an earlier structure succeeded")
            next
        }
        a <- attempt(cand)
        status <- if (a$failed) "ERROR" else if (length(a$warnings) > 0) "OK (with warnings)" else "OK"
        detail <- if (a$failed) a$error else if (length(a$warnings) > 0) paste(a$warnings, collapse = " || ") else "clean fit"
        diag_rows[[i]] <- tibble::tibble(stage = i, structure = cand$spec, status = status,
            optimizer = a$optimizer, seconds = round(a$secs, 2), n_warnings = length(a$warnings), detail = detail)
        if (!a$failed) { chosen <- a$model; chosen_spec <- cand$spec }
    }
    diagnostics <- dplyr::bind_rows(diag_rows)
    if (is.null(chosen)) {
        warning("All structures failed for moderator '", group_var, "'.")
        return(list(data = dat2, VCV = VCV, model = NULL, spec = "ALL FAILED",
                    diagnostics = diagnostics, group_var = group_var))
    }
    list(data = dat2, VCV = VCV, model = chosen, spec = chosen_spec,
         diagnostics = diagnostics, group_var = group_var)
}

# Omnibus among-level test (QM tests all non-intercept coefficients jointly).

    list(QM = res$model$QM, QMp = res$model$QMp, QMdf = res$model$QMdf)
}
```

### Reporting helpers

```{r}
#| label: report_helpers
#| message: false
#| warning: false
# Partial I^2 (long form) for a fitted moderator model.
comp_labels <- c(
    Total                  = "Total",
    identifierStudyId      = "Study",
    identifierEffectSizeID = "Effect size (residual)",
    taxonomySpecies        = "Species (non-phylogenetic)"
)
# orchaRd::i2_ml() does not support the heteroscedastic (HCS) structure that the
# cascade prefers, so for the partial-I² *decomposition* we compute i2_ml on a
# homoscedastic refit of the same data (one variance per random effect). This is
# exactly how the overall synthesis reports partial I² (from the homoscedastic
# intercept models); the heteroscedastic model is retained for the means/Q_M.
i2_homosc_refit <- function(res) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model) || is.null(res$data)) return(NULL)
    d <- res$data
    m <- tryCatch(
        metafor::rma.mv(
            yi = d$yi, V = res$VCV,
            mods = stats::as.formula(paste0("~ ", res$group_var)),
            random = list(~ 1 | identifierStudyId, ~ 1 | identifierEffectSizeID, ~ 1 | taxonomySpecies),
            test = "t", method = "REML", sparse = TRUE, data = d
        ),
        error = function(e) NULL
    )
    if (is.null(m)) return(NULL)
    tryCatch(orchaRd::i2_ml(m), error = function(e) NULL)
}

get_i2_long <- function(res, analysis) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model)) return(NULL)
    v <- tryCatch(orchaRd::i2_ml(res$model), error = function(e) NULL)
    if (is.null(v)) v <- i2_homosc_refit(res)
    if (is.null(v)) return(NULL)
    tibble::tibble(Analysis = analysis, Component = sub("^I2_", "", names(v)), I2 = round(as.numeric(v), 1)) %>%
        mutate(Component = dplyr::recode(Component, !!!comp_labels))
}

# Total I^2: orchaRd on the fitted model, else on the homoscedastic refit (i2_ml
# does not support the HCS structure), else a Higgins typical-variance fallback.
total_i2 <- function(res) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model)) return(NA_real_)
    i2 <- tryCatch(orchaRd::i2_ml(res$model), error = function(e) NULL)
    if (is.null(i2)) i2 <- i2_homosc_refit(res)
    if (!is.null(i2) && any(grepl("Total", names(i2)))) return(as.numeric(i2[grep("Total", names(i2))[1]]))
    m <- res$model; vi <- res$data$vi; wi <- 1 / vi; k <- length(vi)
    s2 <- (k - 1) * sum(wi) / (sum(wi)^2 - sum(wi^2))
    vc <- sum(c(m$sigma2, m$tau2, m$gamma2), na.rm = TRUE)
    100 * vc / (vc + s2)
}

# Per-level marginalised means table.
level_means <- function(res, analysis) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model)) return(NULL)
    mr <- orchaRd::mod_results(res$model, mod = res$group_var, group = "identifierStudyId")
    mr$mod_table %>%
        transmute(Analysis = analysis, Level = name,
                  Estimate = round(estimate, 3),
                  `CI lower` = round(lowerCL, 3), `CI upper` = round(upperCL, 3),
                  `PI lower` = round(lowerPR, 3), `PI upper` = round(upperPR, 3))
}

# Between-level contrasts: each non-intercept model coefficient is the
# difference between that level and the reference level, on the effect-size
# scale. A statistically "distinct" difference would have a CI excluding zero;
# a biologically suggestive one is read from the sign and magnitude even when the
# CI overlaps zero. For log OR models we also report the multiplicative odds
# ratio (exp(difference)) so the contrast is interpretable on the OR scale.
level_contrasts <- function(res, analysis, is_or = FALSE) {
    if (is.null(res) || is.null(res$model)) return(NULL)
    m <- res$model
    nm <- rownames(m$b)
    keep <- !grepl("intrcpt|intercept", nm, ignore.case = TRUE)
    if (!any(keep)) return(NULL)
    ref <- levels(droplevels(factor(res$data[[res$group_var]], levels = levels(res$data[[res$group_var]]))))[1]
    out <- tibble::tibble(
        Analysis = analysis,
        Contrast = paste0(sub(res$group_var, "", nm[keep]), " − ", ref),
        Difference = round(as.numeric(m$b)[keep], 3),
        `CI lower` = round(m$ci.lb[keep], 3),
        `CI upper` = round(m$ci.ub[keep], 3),
        p = round(m$pval[keep], 3)
    )
    if (is_or) {
        out$`Odds ratio` <- round(exp(out$Difference), 2)
        out <- dplyr::relocate(out, `Odds ratio`, .after = Difference)
    }
    out
}

    oc +

}
```

## Biologically Meaningful Level Differences {#sec-biological}

A non-significant $Q_M$ tells us a moderator does not *explain the heterogeneity*, but it does not tell us whether the **direction and magnitude** of the level differences are biologically interesting. Because the point estimates speak to specific hypotheses raised in the main text, we read them here as well — keeping firmly in mind that every contrast CI below overlaps zero, so these are **directional signals, not demonstrated effects**.


### Observer virginity — a signal that runs *against* the original meta-analysis {#sec-bio-virginity}

This is the most noteworthy contrast. Jones & DuVal [@jonesMechanismsSocialInfluence2019] reported — and the present manuscript's discussion repeats as support for the "copy when inexperienced" social-learning strategy — that **virgin (sexually naïve) observers copy more strongly**. Our new extraction shows the **opposite ordering**: virgin observers copied *less* than non-virgins (Hedges' $g$ = `r fmt(vh_yes, 2)` for virgins vs `r fmt(vh_no, 2)` for non-virgins; log OR = `r fmt(vo_yes, 2)` vs `r fmt(vo_no, 2)`). The difference is not statistically distinct (CIs overlap zero), but the *sign* is reversed relative to the classic result. When the new data are pooled with the original Jones & DuVal binary data, the contrast all but disappears (non-virgin OR = `r fmt(exp(vc_no), 2)` vs virgin OR = `r fmt(exp(vc_yes), 2)`).

The takeaway is not that the original finding is wrong, but that the **updated, broader evidence base no longer reproduces the virgin-copies-more pattern**, and the new studies, if anything, point the other way. This is precisely the kind of context-dependence the discussion invokes, and it argues for treating the virginity-conditionality claim with more caution than the original synthesis implied.

### Demonstration cue: copulation versus mere association {#sec-bio-cue}

Within the dominant *visual* modality, restricting to the two well-populated cue types shows copying trends **stronger when the social cue is a copulation than mere association** (Hedges' $g$ = `r fmt(dt_cop, 2)` vs `r fmt(dt_assoc, 2)`). This is the biologically expected direction — a completed copulation is a less ambiguous, higher-value social signal than spatial proximity alone — and it offers a concrete, testable prediction for future work, even though the contrast is not significant here.

### Copying mechanism: generalized copying is not the weaker form {#sec-bio-mechanism}

The introduction motivates *generalized* copying (forming a preference for a phenotype rather than an individual) as the route by which mate-choice copying could drive the cultural transmission of preferences and shape sexual selection. Reassuringly for that argument, generalized copying is **no weaker than individual copying** in the combined data (Hedges' $g$ = `r fmt(mc_gen, 2)` generalized vs `r fmt(mc_ind, 2)` individual) — the evolutionarily consequential form of the behaviour is at least as strong as the individual-recognition form.

### Social-cue modality: a sampling gap, not a result {#sec-bio-modality}

The modality comparison (visual $g$ = `r fmt(mo_vis, 2)`, chemical = `r fmt(mo_che, 2)`, acoustic = `r fmt(mo_aco, 2)`) cannot be read biologically: the chemical and acoustic levels rest on a handful of effect sizes from one to three studies and are confounded with particular species. Its real value is as a **map of where the evidence is missing** — the mate-choice-copying literature is overwhelmingly visual, even though the field's own inclusion criteria explicitly admit chemical and acoustic cues. Broadening sensory sampling is a clear opportunity.

### Interpretation {#sec-interpretation}

Across every model, the pattern is the same: **total heterogeneity remains high and no single moderator's omnibus test is significant.** None of copying mechanism, social-cue modality, demonstration sub-type, or observer virginity reduces the heterogeneity to a degree that the among-level test can detect, on either effect-size scale, and the two combined-dataset extensions confirm this once the legacy data are added. The marginalised level means overlap substantially, and partial $I^2$ stays concentrated at the residual effect-size and species levels — precisely the components the overall synthesis flagged as carrying the bulk of the variance.

This is a genuinely informative null. It tells us that the heterogeneity is **not** explained by the most obvious experimental and ecological axes available in the current data, and it sharpens the case made in the discussion: the moderators that matter are likely finer-grained (specific paradigms, demonstrator quality, ecological context) or interactive, and the dataset is not yet large or balanced enough — particularly for the non-visual modalities and for any moderator absent from the legacy datasets — to resolve them. We therefore present these as **hypothesis-generating** results and echo the discussion's recommendation that future, ideally pre-registered, syntheses test these moderators explicitly as the evidence base grows.

That said, the null heterogeneity result and the [directional level differences](#sec-biological) are two different readings of the same models, and the second is where the biological interest lies. The standout is **observer virginity**: the updated data do not reproduce — and trend against — the virgin-copies-more pattern of the original synthesis, a nuance worth carrying into the discussion even though the contrast is not statistically distinct.

::: {.callout-note}
## Caveats
These models are exploratory and were not pre-registered. Several levels (Chemical and Acoustic modality in particular) rest on very few studies and are confounded with individual species, so their estimates are unstable. Absence of a significant $Q_M$ is *not* evidence that a moderator has no effect — only that the current data cannot resolve one. Finally, where the heteroscedastic (`HCS`) structure was used, `orchaRd`/`metafor` flag that **prediction intervals** for random-slope structures are approximate (the point estimates, confidence intervals, and $Q_M$ are unaffected); the PI columns in the per-level tables should therefore be read as indicative only.
:::
